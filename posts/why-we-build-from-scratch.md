# Why We Build From Scratch

Templates are fast. Custom is forever.

Every week, someone asks us: *"Why don't you just use a template?"* It's a fair question. Templates are cheap, they're quick, and they look good enough on the surface. So why do we insist on building everything from scratch?

## The Template Trap

Templates solve a generic problem generically. They're built for everyone, which means they're optimized for no one. The moment you need something slightly outside the template's assumptions — a custom workflow, an unusual data model, a specific integration — you're fighting the template instead of building your product.

We've seen it dozens of times. A company starts with a template, customizes it for six months, and ends up with something that's harder to maintain than if they'd built from scratch. The technical debt compounds. The workarounds multiply. Eventually, they come to us to rebuild it properly.

## What "From Scratch" Actually Means

Building from scratch doesn't mean reinventing the wheel. We use modern frameworks, battle-tested libraries, and proven patterns. What we don't use is someone else's architectural decisions baked into a theme file.

From scratch means:

- **Your data model**, not a generic one bent to fit
- **Your user flows**, not a template's assumptions about how people navigate
- **Your integration points**, not plugin-based approximations
- **Your design system**, not a theme with your logo swapped in

## The Long-Term Math

A template costs less upfront. Always. But software isn't a one-time purchase — it's an ongoing system. The question isn't *"what does it cost to build?"* but *"what does it cost to operate, maintain, and evolve over the next three years?"*

Custom software built properly has lower maintenance costs, fewer breaking changes, and scales with your business instead of against it.

## When Templates Are Fine

We're not dogmatic. If you need a marketing site and WordPress handles it, use WordPress. If Shopify covers your e-commerce needs, use Shopify.

But when you're building a product — something your team uses daily, something your customers interact with, something that *is* your business — that's when custom matters. That's when you need proper motion.
